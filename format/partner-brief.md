# Partner Brief

> What you take on when you take a seat at the table for an evening.

**No financial contribution to VRC. No sponsorship fee to the OEC.**
The investment is of a different kind.

## What the pitch is for

Your seven minutes have one job: let the ecosystem understand your
core business and where collaboration with you makes sense.

Concretely, the room should walk away knowing:

- **What you do** - your knowledge, your services, your products
- **Who you are** - the humans behind the logo, not the wordmark
- **Why you exist** - the core business in one clear sentence
- **Where you fit** - how you collaborate inside the cloud-native ecosystem
- **When to work with you** - the signal that says "this is the moment to talk"

The Roundtables exist because logos do not build trust. People do.
A pitch that lands gives the room a face, a competence, and a clear
sense of when to pick up the phone.

## What you commit to

A partner who takes a seat at the table brings:

- A prepared seven-minute pitch that lands the five points above
- Time in the moderated Q&A
- Presence for the full 17:00 to 20:00 window, including the
  conversation after the formal part wraps
- A list of five to eight customers or customer-adjacent people
  you personally invite into the room
- A jointly funded share of food, drinks, and any merch for the
  night, carried with the other two partners on stage
- Willingness to be recorded and to have the clip released
- Discretion about the venue, which we share only with confirmed guests

That is it. The investment is presence, preparation, hospitality,
and the cost of feeding the room together with two peers around
the same fire.

## Preparing your pitch

Seven minutes is short. The structure below is one way to use the
time. Adjust to fit your story.

### A simple shape

| Minute       | What you cover                                          |
|--------------|----------------------------------------------------------|
| 0:00 - 1:00  | Who you are. Name, role, time in the ecosystem.          |
| 1:00 - 3:00  | Your core business. Knowledge, services, products.       |
| 3:00 - 5:00  | A real partnership moment. One story, told straight.     |
| 5:00 - 6:00  | Where collaboration with you fits inside the ecosystem.  |
| 6:00 - 7:00  | When to call you. The signal that says "now."            |

### The five questions, made concrete

**1. What you do**
What does your company actually deliver? Concrete services, concrete
products, concrete knowledge. Avoid mission statements. The room
already knows you exist; they want to know what is inside the box.

**2. Who you are**
The humans behind the logo. Who is in the room with the customer
when you work together? What do they care about? What is the
working style? This is the part that wordmarks cannot communicate.

**3. Why you exist**
Your core business, in one sentence. If you have to take two
sentences, it is not your core business yet.

**4. Where you fit**
Show the room how you collaborate. Where do you sit in the
cloud-native stack? Which kinds of orgs do you work alongside?
What does a partnership with you look like in practice?

**5. When to call you**
The most useful sentence in the whole pitch. The signal a customer
or a partner is feeling that says "this is the moment to reach out
to them." Be specific. Vague triggers do not stick.

### What works

- One real partnership story, told straight, names changed if needed
- One specific number, fact, or technical detail that grounds the talk
- A clear "when to call us" line the room can repeat to a colleague
- A working style described in plain words, not adjectives

### What does not

- The full company pitch deck
- Anything that reads like marketing copy
- "We help companies achieve their goals" or any version of it
- Slides with eight bullets and forty words each
- A demo. There is no time. The recording is not the place either.

### On slides

Slides are optional. Three is plenty. The voice carries further when
there is nothing to read along to.

If you do use them, one suggestion: one slide for your name and role,
one slide for the partnership story, one slide for "when to call us."
Everything else is in the speaking.

## Bringing listeners

Five to eight people you personally invite, with a personal note.
Not a marketing list. People you want in the room because the
conversation will serve them, and they will serve the conversation.

The customers do not have to be your customers. Someone working at a
company in your space, someone you have been wanting to talk to, a
peer from a partner company - all count. The Roundtables work because
the room is composed by hand, not by registration form.

## After the evening

You get your seven-minute clip, edited and ready to use wherever
serves you - on your site, in a post, on a careers page, in front
of a customer who wants to see the face behind the wordmark.

The clip is the artefact that keeps working long after the evening
ends. Partners often find that one well-prepared seven-minute pitch
becomes their default "this is who we are" video for the next year.

## What VRC carries

While you bring the pitch, the people, and the hospitality share,
VRC carries:

- Choosing the three voices on stage (curating the season's cast)
- Coaching each speaker through pitch prep, if useful
- Holding the moderator's seat for the formal hour
- The meetup group, the guest list, the day-of logistics
- Recording, editing, publishing the clips
- The format itself, across the seasons

If you want a pitch prep call, ask. It is free, it is included, and
most partners take one.

## What the venue host carries

Each Roundtable is held at a venue in Zurich, offered by a partner who
believes in what the Collective is building. The host gets:

- A quiet thank-you in the welcome
- A line in the edition entry on GitHub
- Discretion - the venue is not promoted publicly

Hosting is the signal. We do not write a sponsorship logo on the door.
